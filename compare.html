The

Roast

Regular Roasting

involves heating green coffee beans until they undergo a series of chemical changes, transforming into the familiar brown beans we use for brewing. During roasting, the beans expand, lose moisture, and develop complex flavours and aromas.

Torrefacto Roasting

is a technique which originates from Spain, involving the addition of sugar during roasting, producing a caramelised flavour. Coffee beans used in kopi are not just roasted with sugar, but also butter - resulting in a creamy, rounded taste!

The

Brew

Espresso brewing

involves forcing hot water through finely ground coffee beans at high pressure, resulting in a concentrated shot with a rich flavor, intense aroma, and a layer of crema. It's a highly versatile method, and can be done with various types of machines.

Sock brewing

involves steeping coarse coffee grounds in hot water using a cloth filter resembling a sock, allowing for big batches of coffee to be made at once. Hand-pulling the coffee makes the coffee silky-smooth, while remaining inexpensive, with minimal equipment.

The

Cup...?

Takeaway cups

come in plastic and paper varieties, with lids to ensure mess-free drinking on the go. Did you know that disposable coffee cups were only popularised in the early 1900s, after the Spanish flu made people more aware of the spread of germs and viruses?

Plastic bags

are often used for takeaway kopi - they’re cheap, lightweight and disposable, making them extremely practical. Although they’re becoming less common amidst increasing environmental awareness, they have since become an icon of Singapore’s kopi culture.

With specialty coffee and chain cafes on the rise in Singapore, could kopi culture be at risk of being left behind?

Yeah, I mean, kopi is great - but more and more youngsters like me prefer boutique cafes and specialty coffee!

Sure, it’s definitely more expensive, but the experience is just so much better!

There’s wi-fi, so I can work and study - and not to mention, they’re air-conditioned! What a lifesaver in Singapore’s sweltering heat.

And, I get to have my coffee with plant milks - something I could never have with traditional kopi!

These youngsters don’t know what they’re missing out!

Kopi is cheap and good - why waste your money on fancy, watered down coffees, when you could have a strong cup of kopi for a fraction of the price?

And I can promise you - kopi is here to stay! Even with the rise of specialty coffee shops and chain cafes, kopi still makes up 70% of coffee consumption in Singapore!

There is so much culture in every cup of kopi - we shouldn’t take it for granted!

Both sides make compelling arguments - but perhaps we can work towards a future where they can coexist - and possibly even make each other better?

a cafe with a traditional twist

Butter Bean

Butter Bean, for instance, offers a diverse menu including both kopis and lattes, offering typical cafe amenities like air-conditioning and Wi-Fi, while maintaining an element of authenticity by serving kopi and other traditional Singaporean dishes.

Coffee that keeps up with the times

Generation Coffee

Similarly, ‘traditional’ kopi shops like Generation Coffee are adapting, selling both affordable kopi and specialty coffee from humble hawker stalls.
